07 What your genes are telling us
Plain-language guide · GenVue findings + verified against raw Ancestry DNA (664,421 SNPs)
How to read this section
Genes come in pairs — one from each parent. When we say "one copy" (heterozygous), it means one parent passed down a variant. "Two copies" (homozygous) means both did.

Each gene below has a real impact on how your body works day-to-day, but none of these are diseases — they're tendencies. Think of them like inheriting your dad's height or your mom's eye color, except these affect things like how you handle stress, how your body uses vitamins, or how you respond to certain medications. Knowing them lets us play to your strengths and protect your weak spots.

Pay attention — actively shaping your care. Worth knowing — adjust some habits. Good news — you got lucky here.
MTHFR — the folate processor
One copy of the variant · rs1801133 (C677T) · ~1 in 4 people have this
Pay attention
The simple version: Your body needs folate (vitamin B9) for thousands of jobs — repairing DNA, making brain chemicals, clearing out toxins, building healthy blood cells. But folate from food and most vitamins comes in a "raw" form your body has to convert before it can use it. Your MTHFR gene makes the converter.

What's different about yours: Your converter runs at about 65% efficiency instead of 100%. It's not broken — it just works slower than average. About a quarter of people have this exact variant.

Why this matters for your bones: When folate doesn't get processed properly, a substance called homocysteine builds up in your blood. Elevated homocysteine actually weakens the collagen scaffolding inside your bones — picture rebar inside concrete. Your bone density (the concrete) might look one way on the DEXA, but if the rebar is weakened, the bone is more brittle than the numbers suggest. Given your osteoporosis diagnosis, this is a direct fixable factor.

Why this matters for your heart: The same homocysteine buildup also damages blood vessel walls and contributes to plaque buildup. You already have moderate coronary calcification, so cleaning this up is real prevention work, not just optimization.

The folic acid trap: Most cheap multivitamins, fortified breads, cereals, and orange juice contain "folic acid" — that's the synthetic raw form. With your variant, that synthetic version actually blocks the receptors your body needs to absorb the good stuff. It can make things worse instead of better. You want "methylfolate" or "5-MTHF" on the label — that's the pre-converted form your body can use immediately.

One thing to flag with doctors: If you're ever prescribed methotrexate (used for arthritis or some cancers), they need to know about this variant — it changes how the drug behaves. Same goes for some antidepressants.

What to do: Take Thorne Methyl-Guard Plus daily (it has the right form of folate plus B12 and B6 to support the whole pathway). Read labels — avoid anything that says "folic acid." Ask Dr. Gulati to add a homocysteine blood test next time — we want it under 10. Check your current vitamins and toss any with synthetic folic acid.
COMT — your stress chemical cleanup crew
Both copies are the slow version · rs4680 (Val158Met) · this is the slowest possible setting
Pay attention
The simple version: When something stressful happens, your brain releases adrenaline, dopamine, and other "alert" chemicals. After the stressful moment passes, you need to clean those chemicals back up to feel calm again. The COMT gene makes the cleanup crew. Your COMT also clears used-up estrogen out of your system.

What's different about yours: You inherited the slow cleanup version from both of your parents — so your cleanup crew works at about a quarter of normal speed. This is the slowest possible setting. About one in three people have at least one copy; getting both copies (like you) is less common.

Why this explains a lot: Researchers actually call this the "worrier" genotype — and it's not an insult. Slow cleanup means stress chemicals linger longer. When things are calm, this is actually an advantage — slow-COMT people tend to have stronger focus, better memory, and they don't forget the important details. But when life gets stressful, the cleanup crew falls behind, and that's when anxiety, racing thoughts, and panic attacks can build up faster than you can recover from them. Your panic disorder since 2016 and your anxiety flares aren't a personal weakness — they're partly a wiring difference.

Why this might have affected your bones: Slow estrogen cleanup through your perimenopausal years may have created an unusual hormone pattern as you went through menopause. Estrogen is what protects bone density in women — and the way your body handled estrogen on the way out may have contributed to where you are now.

Why caffeine hits you harder: Caffeine boosts the same alert chemicals your slow COMT can't clean up fast enough. So one cup of coffee in you can feel like two cups in someone else, and the jittery effect lasts longer. Same with alcohol and dark chocolate to a lesser degree.

The MTHFR-COMT compound effect: Your COMT cleanup crew runs on a chemical called SAM, which is made by your methylation system (MTHFR). So your slow MTHFR is making your already-slow COMT even slower. The good news: fixing one helps the other. The Methyl-Guard Plus (for MTHFR) actually pulls double duty here.

CFH — eye protection regulator
One copy of the variant · rs1061170 · ~1 in 4 people have this · ClinVar: pathogenic risk factor
Worth knowing
The simple version: Your eye's retina needs careful protection from your immune system — too much immune activity in there causes inflammation that damages your central vision over time. The CFH gene makes a protein that acts like a referee, telling your immune system "stand down" inside the retina.

What's different about yours: Your referee isn't quite as effective as it should be. The immune system runs slightly hot in your retina, causing slow, low-grade inflammation under the macula (the center of your vision used for reading, driving, and seeing faces).

What this puts you at risk for: Age-related macular degeneration, called AMD. It's one of the leading causes of vision loss in people over 60. Here's the scary part — it's silent until significant damage is done. You don't get blurry vision early on. Your peripheral vision stays fine. By the time you notice central vision changes, the damage is often hard to reverse.

Why early detection completely changes the outcome: AMD comes in two flavors. The "dry" form moves slowly over years — manageable. The "wet" form, where abnormal blood vessels grow under the retina, can take vision in weeks. But — and this is the key — if you catch wet AMD early, there are eye injections that can completely halt the progression. The catch is, you only catch it early if someone's actively looking, which means a real dilated eye exam by an ophthalmologist (the medical doctor for eyes), not just a glasses-prescription visit at the optometrist.

The good news: There's a specific vitamin formula called AREDS2 — based on a major clinical trial — that's been proven to slow AMD progression by about 25% in people with exactly your genetic profile. It's the rare case where a specific supplement has gold-standard scientific evidence behind it.

What to do: Schedule a dilated eye exam with an ophthalmologist (an MD, not an optometrist) — and put it on the annual calendar going forward. Start the Vision Defender AREDS2 supplement (the formulation matters — it has to be exactly the AREDS2 mix). The omega-3 in the CoQ10/Omega combo also helps protect your retina.
ADD1 — your salt sensitivity dial
One copy of the variant · rs4961 · ~1 in 5 people have this
Worth knowing
The simple version: When you eat salty food, your kidneys decide how much salt to keep and how much to flush out in urine. The ADD1 gene helps regulate that decision. People with your variant hold onto more salt than average — which means salt raises your blood pressure more than it would for most people.

What this means in practice: The same Chinese takeout, frozen meal, or salty snack that gives a friend a small temporary blood pressure bump might give you a bigger and longer-lasting one. It's not that you have high blood pressure — your readings are actually beautiful, around 117/74. It's that salt is a more powerful lever for you than for most people, in both directions. If you ever needed to bring your BP down, cutting sodium would work especially well for you. And if you ever started loading up on salt, your BP would climb faster than someone else's.

The anxiety connection: Remember that high reading you had during the September 2025 anxiety flare (167/90)? Anxiety raises BP. Salt raises BP. Your variant amplifies salt's effect. So a stressful day plus a salty dinner is a stack of three things working together. Worth knowing.

The flip side — potassium is your superpower: Potassium does the opposite of sodium at the kidney level. A diet rich in potassium (avocado, sweet potato, banana, leafy greens, salmon) actively cancels out the salt-retention mechanism your variant creates. So instead of just "eat less salt," the better framing is "keep salt moderate AND make sure you're getting plenty of potassium-rich foods every day."

If you ever need a BP medication: The class called diuretics (specifically thiazides and loop diuretics like furosemide) work especially well for people with your variant. Worth telling any doctor who's prescribing.

What to do: Aim for under 1,500 mg of sodium per day (the average American eats 3,400 mg). Build potassium into every day — avocado, sweet potato, banana, salmon, leafy greens. Track BP at home occasionally, especially after salty restaurant meals or stressful weeks. Magnesium glycinate also helps.
AMPD1 — muscle energy recycling
One copy of the variant · rs17602729 · uncommon (~4% of people)
Worth knowing
The simple version: When your muscles work hard, they burn through energy and need to recycle the spent fuel back into usable energy. The AMPD1 gene helps run that recycling process inside muscle cells. People with two copies of your variant can have noticeable exercise problems; people with one copy (you) usually have a milder version.

What this means for you: You may notice you tire a bit faster during exercise than you'd expect, and recovery between workouts may take longer. Soreness might be more pronounced. It's not a sign you should avoid exercise — it's a sign that "lots of moderate exercise with good recovery" works better for you than "occasional intense sessions."

Why this matters specifically right now: Resistance training is the single most powerful non-medication thing you can do for your osteoporosis. Bones get stronger when they're loaded — pulling and pushing against weight or gravity tells the body "we need to keep this bone strong." The trick for you is doing it in a way that respects your recycling speed: shorter sessions, lighter weights, more frequency, full rest days between. You already do pilates, which is excellent. Adding some specific weight-bearing work — even with bands or your own body weight — for hips and spine 2 or 3 times a week would give your femoral neck (the bone area that's currently most affected) the signal it needs to rebuild.

The CoQ10 connection: The CoQ10 supplement does double duty here — it's the spark plug of your cells' energy factories. It helps your muscles work more efficiently within the constraints your variant creates. Your statin also depletes CoQ10, so this is a layered benefit.

APOE — the brain protector you won the lottery on
Both copies of the protective version · rs7412 (ApoE2/E2) · only ~1 in 100 people have this
Good news — rare and protective
The simple version: APOE is a protein that does two big jobs — it helps your body move cholesterol around in your bloodstream, and it helps your brain clear out a sticky waste protein called amyloid-beta. Amyloid-beta buildup is what forms the plaques you've heard about in Alzheimer's disease.

The three versions of this gene: Everyone inherits one of three versions from each parent. ApoE2 (yours) is the rare protective one. ApoE3 is the most common — it's neutral. ApoE4 is the well-known risk version — having one copy roughly doubles your risk of Alzheimer's, and two copies multiplies it by 8 to 12 times. You inherited ApoE2 from both your mom and your dad. This is rare — only about 1 in 100 people are ApoE2/E2.

What this means for your brain: Your version of APOE clears amyloid-beta significantly more efficiently than the other versions. That doesn't make you immune to Alzheimer's — many factors contribute, including sleep, exercise, social engagement, and overall vascular health — but you're starting with a real biological advantage. Your brain is, all else equal, better equipped to handle aging.

What this means for your cholesterol: ApoE2 people generally have naturally lower LDL cholesterol than average — and your numbers bear this out beautifully. There's one thing to watch for: very rarely, ApoE2/E2 combined with other metabolic stress can cause an unusual lipid pattern called "type 3 hyperlipoproteinemia." Your current panel shows zero sign of this, but it's why annual lipid checks matter.

You also respond well to statins: ApoE2 people tend to be excellent statin responders — and your atorvastatin is doing exactly what it should. LDL of 44 is genuinely outstanding.

This deserves a moment of celebration. Most genetic findings tell us something to manage. This one tells us about something you got right at the genetic lottery. The therapy work you're doing on stress, the exercise routine, the sleep — all of those amplify this advantage further.

SLCO1B1 — your statin handler
One copy of the *5 variant · rs4149056 (T/C heterozygous) · this is why your dose got reduced
Pay attention
The simple version: When you take a statin, your liver has to grab the medication out of your bloodstream and bring it inside the liver cells where it does its job. The SLCO1B1 gene makes the "doorway" protein on liver cells that lets statins in. If the doorway works at full speed, statins get pulled out of your bloodstream quickly. If the doorway is slower, statin levels stay higher in your blood for longer — which means the same dose hits your muscles harder.

What's different about yours: You inherited one slow doorway gene (called the *5 variant) from one parent, and one normal doorway gene from the other. Worth knowing
The simple version: A specific stretch of DNA on chromosome 9 (called the 9p21 locus) is the single most-studied genetic risk factor for coronary artery disease. It doesn't make a protein the way most genes do — instead it produces regulatory RNA that influences how blood vessel cells age, divide, and respond to inflammation. Carriers of the variant tend to develop coronary plaque earlier and faster.

What's different about yours: You inherited the risk version from both parents — what's called the G/G genotype at this position. Studies in multiple populations (American, European, Asian, Indian, Saudi) consistently show that G/G carriers have roughly 1.5 to 2 times the coronary artery disease risk of A/A carriers. Roughly 25% of people are G/G; another 50% are A/G; the remaining 25% are the protective A/A.
