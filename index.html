MTHFR C677T — heterozygous  genotype: TC
rs1801133  ·  Chr 1:11856378  ·  c.665C>T (p.Ala222Val)  ·  CADD 25.5  ·  Freq 24.5%
High priority
ClinVar: Conflicting interpretations / risk factor Expert panel reviewed · 10 submitters OMIM: 607093.0003
What this gene does: MTHFR (methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase) is the enzyme responsible for the critical conversion of dietary folate into its biologically active form: 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF). This active folate is the key methyl donor that powers the methylation cycle — the body's fundamental biochemical on/off switch governing DNA synthesis and repair, neurotransmitter production, detoxification, immune regulation, and homocysteine clearance.

What this variant does: The C677T polymorphism (also known as the "thermolabile" variant, or A222V at the protein level) encodes a form of the MTHFR enzyme that is structurally less stable at body temperature, causing it to unfold and lose activity. As a heterozygous carrier (one copy), Cathy's enzyme efficiency is reduced by approximately 35% compared to wildtype. This is clinically meaningful but less severe than the TT homozygous state (60–70% reduction).

The folic acid problem: Standard synthetic folic acid — in fortified breads, cereals, orange juice, prenatal vitamins, and most cheap multivitamins — must pass through MTHFR to be converted into 5-MTHF. With reduced MTHFR activity, unmetabolized folic acid (UMFA) accumulates and can competitively block folate receptors, paradoxically worsening the deficiency it was meant to treat. This is why the supplement protocol specifies methylfolate (5-MTHF) — the pre-converted active form that bypasses MTHFR entirely.

Why this matters for bone health: When folate metabolism is impaired, homocysteine cannot be remethylated back to methionine and accumulates. Elevated homocysteine is an independent risk factor for osteoporotic fractures — it directly impairs bone collagen cross-linking, reducing bone matrix quality independent of density measurements. Given Cathy's DEXA finding, testing and treating homocysteine is a direct intervention on fracture risk.

Why this matters cardiovascularly: Elevated homocysteine is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease (endothelial damage, atherosclerosis) and venous thrombosis. Cathy already has established coronary atherosclerosis — homocysteine optimization is residual risk reduction.

Drug interactions to flag: Methotrexate works by inhibiting folate metabolism — if ever prescribed (for rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis, or cancer), providers must know her MTHFR status. Also relevant: MTHFR variant affects SSRI response since serotonin synthesis depends on methylation.

Cathy's MCV of 91 is reassuring: MTHFR-driven folate/B12 deficiency classically causes macrocytic anemia with elevated MCV. However, functional methylation deficits can exist for years before CBC changes — only homocysteine testing confirms actual impact.

COMT Val158Met — homozygous  genotype: AA (slow-slow)
rs4680  ·  Chr 22:19951271  ·  c.472G>A (p.Val158Met)  ·  CADD 13.15  ·  Freq 36.9%
High priority
ClinVar: Drug response / functional polymorphism Expert panel reviewed · 3 submitters OMIM: 116790.0001 598 PubMed publications
What this gene does: COMT (catechol-O-methyltransferase) is a key enzyme in the catecholamine degradation pathway. It breaks down dopamine, epinephrine (adrenaline), norepinephrine, and — critically — catechol estrogens (metabolized forms of estradiol). COMT uses SAM (S-adenosylmethionine) as a methyl donor, which means it is directly dependent on the methylation cycle also affected by the MTHFR variant. These two variants compound each other.

What this variant does: The Val158Met substitution reduces COMT enzyme thermostability. Homozygous AA (two copies) produces the slowest COMT activity — approximately 3–4× lower than the high-activity GG genotype. Cathy carries the slowest possible COMT configuration. This is a codominant variant, meaning two slow copies produce a meaningfully different phenotype than one.

The "worrier" phenotype — and what we see clinically: Because dopamine is cleared more slowly in the prefrontal cortex, slow-COMT individuals show stronger working memory and sustained attention under calm conditions — but under high stress, the cortex becomes flooded with catecholamines that cannot clear fast enough, impairing executive function and amplifying the stress response. This is exactly the pattern Cathy's 8-year record describes: long-standing panic disorder since 2016, GAD, heightened emotional memory, amplified response to major life stressors (family deaths in 2024, marital stress 2025). Her anxiety is not "just stress" — it is at least partly a genetic predisposition.

Estrogen and bone health connection: COMT clears catechol estrogens — particularly 4-hydroxyestradiol. When COMT is slow, these metabolites linger. More importantly in context: estrogen is the primary regulator of osteoclast activity in women. Post-menopausal estrogen decline removes this brake. Slow estrogen clearance through perimenopause may have altered Cathy's hormonal trajectory in ways that compounded bone density loss. This is a plausible contributing mechanism to her femoral neck T-score of −2.7.

Caffeine sensitivity: Caffeine indirectly elevates dopamine and norepinephrine — already slow to clear. This produces prolonged stimulant effects and anxiety amplification most people don't experience. Similarly: alcohol, high-catechol foods, and certain stimulants.

The MTHFR–COMT compounding effect: COMT requires SAM as its methyl donor — SAM production is impaired by poor methylation from MTHFR dysfunction. Her two high-priority variants amplify each other. Supporting methylation with 5-MTHF + methyl-B12 simultaneously helps COMT function. Magnesium is a direct COMT cofactor.

CFH His402Tyr — heterozygous  genotype: TC
rs1061170  ·  Chr 1:196659237  ·  p.His402=/Tyr  ·  Freq 26.7%  ·  OMIM: 134370.0008
Moderate-High

What this gene does: Complement Factor H (CFH) is a critical regulator of the alternative complement pathway. In the retina, tight regulation of complement activity is essential — the retina has minimal blood supply and cannot afford inflammatory damage. CFH acts as the primary brake on complement activation in retinal tissue.

What this variant does: The His402Tyr variant alters a key binding domain in the CFH protein, reducing its ability to bind CRP and heparin in the extracellular matrix of the retina. This weakens CFH's regulatory function and allows the complement cascade to proceed unchecked in Bruch's membrane beneath the retinal pigment epithelium. The result: chronic, low-grade complement-mediated inflammation in the macula that accumulates over decades.

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) — the central concern: AMD is a leading cause of irreversible vision loss in adults over 60, affecting central (macular) vision needed for reading, driving, recognizing faces. It does not affect peripheral or night vision in its early stages — which is why it can progress silently for years. Two forms:
  • Dry AMD (85–90%): Yellow drusen deposits, slow progressive photoreceptor loss over years.
  • Wet AMD (10–15%): Abnormal blood vessels leak beneath the macula. Vision loss can occur within weeks. Most severe AMD blindness.

Why annual dilated eye exams are non-negotiable: AMD produces no early symptoms. A dilated retinal exam by an ophthalmologist (not merely an optometrist refraction) allows visualization of drusen, RPE changes, early neovascularization. When caught early, anti-VEGF injections can halt wet AMD progression. Once central vision is lost, it cannot be restored.

Gap in current record: Cathy's file shows regular optometry visits for prescription and she has had cataract surgery (2020), but no dedicated annual dilated retinal exam with ophthalmology is documented. This should change.

The AREDS2 evidence: The AREDS2 NIH trial showed the specific formulation (lutein 10mg + zeaxanthin 2mg + vit C 500mg + vit E 400 IU + zinc 25mg + copper 2mg) reduces progression to advanced AMD by ~25% in at-risk patients. Level I evidence for this exact genetic risk profile.

What this gene does: ADD1 (alpha-adducin) encodes a cytoskeletal protein expressed in kidney tubular cells. It regulates the Na+/K+-ATPase pump — the primary transport protein responsible for sodium reabsorption from urine. More reabsorption = higher blood sodium = more fluid retention = higher BP.

What this variant does: The Gly460Trp substitution increases the activity of Na+/K+-ATPase in the kidney. Cathy's kidneys retain more sodium per unit of dietary intake than average. APOE rs7412 — homozygous ApoE2  genotype: TT (ApoE2/E2)
rs7412  ·  Chr 19:45412079  ·  c.526C>T (p.Arg176Cys)  ·  CADD 25.1  ·  Freq 7.5%
Protective
ClinVar: Drug response (generally protective) OMIM: 107741.0001 Homozygous — ~0.5–1% of population
What this gene does: APOE encodes apolipoprotein E — central to lipid transport and cholesterol clearance, and critically, to brain health. Expressed in brain tissue, ApoE plays a role in neuronal repair, synaptic plasticity, and clearing amyloid-beta protein (the primary constituent of Alzheimer's plaques).

The three APOE variants: ApoE2 (rs7412 T allele), ApoE3 (most common), ApoE4 (high-risk). ApoE4 is the most studied genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's — one copy doubles risk; two copies increases risk 8–12×. ApoE2 is at the opposite end of the spectrum: significantly reduced Alzheimer's risk.

Cathy's ApoE2/E2 genotype: Rare — only ~0.5–1% of the population. This is her most genetically favorable finding. ApoE2/E2 individuals have meaningfully reduced amyloid-beta accumulation. ApoE2 binds amyloid-beta more effectively than ApoE4, facilitating clearance before plaque formation. This does not make someone immune to Alzheimer's but it represents a significant biological advantage.

The lipid monitoring caveat: ApoE2 has lower affinity for LDL receptors than ApoE3/E4. Generally this means ApoE2 individuals tend to have lower LDL — another favorable finding, and Cathy's beautiful lipid numbers bear this out.
