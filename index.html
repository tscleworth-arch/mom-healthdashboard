MTHFR C677T — heterozygous  genotype: TC
rs1801133  ·  Chromosome 1:11856378  ·  Gene: MTHFR  ·  Variant: c.665C>T (p.Ala222Val)  ·  CADD score: 25.5  ·  Freq: 24.5%
High priority
ClinVar: Conflicting interpretations / risk factor Expert panel reviewed · 10 submitters OMIM: 607093.0003
What this gene does: MTHFR (methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase) is the enzyme responsible for the critical conversion of dietary folate into its biologically active form: 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF). This active folate is the key methyl donor that powers the methylation cycle — the body's fundamental biochemical on/off switch governing DNA synthesis and repair, neurotransmitter production, detoxification, immune regulation, and homocysteine clearance.

What this variant does: The C677T polymorphism (also known as the "thermolabile" variant, or A222V at the protein level) encodes a form of the MTHFR enzyme that is structurally less stable at body temperature, causing it to unfold and lose activity. As a heterozygous carrier (one copy), Cathy's enzyme efficiency is reduced by approximately 35% compared to the wildtype. This is clinically meaningful but less severe than the TT homozygous state (two copies), which reduces efficiency by 60–70%.

The folic acid problem: Standard synthetic folic acid — found in fortified breads, cereals, orange juice, prenatal vitamins, and most cheap multivitamins — must pass through MTHFR to be converted into 5-MTHF before the body can use it. With reduced MTHFR activity, unmetabolized folic acid (UMFA) accumulates and can actually competitively block the folate receptors, paradoxically worsening the deficiency it was meant to treat. This is why the supplement protocol specifies methylfolate (5-MTHF) — the pre-converted active form that bypasses the MTHFR step entirely.

Downstream consequences — homocysteine: When folate metabolism is impaired, the amino acid homocysteine cannot be remethylated back to methionine and accumulates in the blood. Elevated homocysteine (hyperhomocysteinemia) is an independent risk factor for: cardiovascular disease (endothelial damage, atherosclerosis), venous thrombosis, and — critically given her DEXA results — osteoporotic fractures. Elevated homocysteine directly impairs bone collagen cross-linking, reducing bone matrix quality independent of bone density measurements. Her MCV of 91 (mid-range, normal) is reassuring since MTHFR-driven B12/folate deficiency classically causes macrocytic anemia with elevated MCV. However, functional methylation deficits can be present for years before CBC changes appear. Only a serum homocysteine test will confirm the actual impact.

Drug interactions to know: Methotrexate (used for rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis, and cancer) works by inhibiting folate metabolism — folate supplementation can reduce methotrexate toxicity in inflammatory conditions but may interfere with its anti-cancer mechanism. If Cathy is ever prescribed methotrexate for any reason, her provider must be informed of MTHFR status and current methylfolate supplementation. Also relevant: the MTHFR variant affects response to certain antidepressants (SSRIs) since serotonin synthesis depends on methylation.

ClinVar-associated conditions for this variant: Gastrointestinal stromal tumor risk (conflicting evidence), neoplasm of stomach (susceptibility), neural tube defects (folate-sensitive), venous thrombosis, and MTHFR deficiency thermolabile type. The clinical significance is listed as "conflicting interpretations of pathogenicity" — meaning experts disagree on severity, which is typical for common functional polymorphisms like this one. It should be treated as a meaningful risk modifier, not a disease-causing mutation.

COMT Val158Met — homozygous  genotype: AA (slow-slow)
rs4680  ·  Chromosome 22:19951271  ·  Gene: COMT  ·  Variant: c.472G>A (p.Val158Met)  ·  CADD score: 13.15  ·  Freq: 36.9%
High priority
ClinVar: Drug response / functional polymorphism Expert panel reviewed · 3 submitters OMIM: 116790.0001 598 PubMed publications
What this gene does: COMT (catechol-O-methyltransferase) is a key enzyme in the catecholamine degradation pathway. It breaks down dopamine, epinephrine (adrenaline), norepinephrine, and — critically — catechol estrogens (the metabolized forms of estradiol and estrone produced in peripheral tissues). COMT uses SAM (S-adenosylmethionine) as a methyl donor, which means it is directly dependent on the methylation cycle also affected by the MTHFR variant. These two variants compound each other.

What this variant does: The Val158Met polymorphism substitutes a methionine for a valine at position 158, reducing COMT enzyme thermostability. Homozygous AA (two copies of the Met allele) produces the slowest COMT activity — approximately 3–4x lower enzyme activity than the high-activity GG genotype. This is a codominant variant, meaning two slow copies produce a meaningfully different phenotype than one. Cathy carries the slowest possible COMT configuration.

Neurological implications — the "worrier" genotype: Because dopamine is cleared more slowly in the prefrontal cortex, slow-COMT individuals typically demonstrate stronger working memory, better focused attention under low-stress conditions, and more persistent emotional memories. However, under high stress, the prefrontal cortex becomes flooded with dopamine and norepinephrine that cannot be cleared fast enough, impairing executive function and amplifying the stress response. This is sometimes called the "warrior vs. worrier" distinction — slow COMT provides cognitive advantages in stable environments but vulnerability under pressure. She is likely more sensitive to stressful events, may have a harder time "letting go" of negative memories, and may experience anxiety in ways that feel disproportionate to others.

Estrogen and bone health implications: This is the COMT connection that directly ties into her DEXA findings. COMT is responsible for clearing catechol estrogens — particularly 4-hydroxyestradiol, a reactive estrogen metabolite. When COMT is slow, these metabolites linger and can create oxidative stress. More importantly in the context of bone health: estrogen is the primary regulator of osteoclast (bone-removing cell) activity in women. Post-menopausal estrogen decline removes this brake, causing accelerated bone resorption. Slow estrogen clearance through the premenopausal years may have altered her hormonal trajectory through perimenopause in ways that compounded bone density loss. This is a plausible contributing mechanism to her femoral neck T-score of −2.7.

Caffeine and substance sensitivity: Caffeine inhibits adenosine receptors and indirectly elevates dopamine and norepinephrine — compounds that are already slow to clear with COMT. This creates a prolonged stimulant effect and heightened anxiety response to caffeine that most people don't experience. Similarly, alcohol, high-catechol foods (dark chocolate, coffee, certain teas, cruciferous vegetables in very large amounts), and dopaminergic medications will all have amplified or prolonged effects.

Pharmacogenomic relevance: ClinVar records nicotine response (efficacy) as an associated drug response — COMT genotype affects how well nicotine replacement therapy works. The variant is also referenced in schizophrenia research (though the effect size is small at the population level). More practically: opioid pain medications and certain antidepressants (particularly those affecting dopamine or norepinephrine) may require dose adjustments given altered catecholamine clearance.

The MTHFR-COMT compounding effect: COMT requires SAM (S-adenosylmethionine) as its methyl donor — and SAM production is directly impaired by poor methylation from MTHFR dysfunction. This means her two high-priority variants amplify each other: MTHFR reduces methyl availability, which further impairs an already slow COMT. Supporting methylation with 5-MTHF + methyl-B12 (the MTHFR protocol) simultaneously helps COMT function. Magnesium is a direct COMT cofactor and should be prioritized.

CFH His402Tyr — heterozygous  genotype: TC
rs1061170  ·  Chromosome 1:196659237  ·  Gene: CFH  ·  Variant: c.1204C= (p.His402=)  ·  Freq: 26.7%  ·  OMIM: 134370.0008
Moderate-High
ClinVar: Pathogenic, risk factor Expert reviewed · 1 submitter 301 PubMed publications Autosomal dominant — may manifest as carrier
What this gene does: Complement Factor H (CFH) is a critical regulator of the alternative pathway of the complement immune system. The complement system is a cascade of proteins that helps clear pathogens, dead cells, and cellular debris. In the retina, tight regulation of complement activity is essential — the retina has minimal blood supply and cannot afford inflammatory damage. CFH acts as the primary brake on complement activation in retinal tissue, preventing the immune system from attacking the retina's own cells.

What this variant does: The His402Tyr variant (histidine to tyrosine at position 402) alters a key binding domain in the CFH protein, reducing its ability to bind to C-reactive protein (CRP) and heparin in the extracellular matrix of the retina. This weakens CFH's regulatory function and allows the complement cascade to proceed unchecked in retinal tissue — particularly in Bruch's membrane beneath the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE). The result is chronic, low-grade complement-mediated inflammation in the macula that accumulates over decades.

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD): AMD is a leading cause of irreversible vision loss in adults over 60, affecting central (macular) vision needed for reading, driving, recognizing faces, and detailed work. It does not affect peripheral vision or night vision in its early stages, which is exactly why it can progress silently for years before the person notices anything is wrong. AMD occurs in two forms:
  • Dry AMD (85–90% of cases): Characterized by accumulation of yellowish deposits called drusen beneath the retina, and slow progressive loss of photoreceptors. Vision loss is gradual, often over years to decades.
  • Wet AMD (10–15% of cases): Characterized by growth of abnormal, fragile blood vessels beneath the macula (choroidal neovascularization) that leak blood and fluid. This form progresses rapidly — vision loss can occur within weeks. It is the form responsible for most severe AMD-related blindness.

Basal laminar drusen: This variant is also classified as a pathogenic risk factor for basal laminar drusen — a specific type of drusen deposit in which abnormal material collects between the retinal pigment epithelium and Bruch's membrane. These are associated with early AMD development and are a precursor marker for disease progression.

Autosomal dominant note: Because the conditions associated with this variant (AMD and basal laminar drusen) have autosomal dominant inheritance patterns, carrying even one copy of the risk allele (as Cathy does, heterozygous) is sufficient to confer meaningful increased risk — she is not simply a "carrier" in the recessive sense. Risk is elevated, though penetrance is incomplete (not everyone with the variant develops AMD).

Why annual dilated eye exams are non-negotiable: AMD produces no pain, no early symptoms, and no changes visible to the person until significant macular damage has occurred. A dilated retinal exam by an ophthalmologist (not merely a standard optometrist refraction exam) allows visualization of drusen, RPE changes, and early neovascularization. When caught early — even pre-symptomatically — anti-VEGF injections can halt wet AMD progression. Once central vision is lost from advanced AMD, it cannot be restored. The exam is the only intervention that changes the outcome.

The AREDS2 connection: The Age-Related Eye Disease Study 2 (AREDS2) was a landmark 5-year NIH clinical trial specifically designed to test whether nutritional supplementation could slow AMD progression in genetically at-risk patients. The trial demonstrated that the specific AREDS2 formulation — lutein 10mg + zeaxanthin 2mg + vitamin C 500mg + vitamin E 400 IU + zinc 25mg + copper 2mg — reduced the risk of progression to advanced AMD by approximately 25% in at-risk patients. This is one of the few cases in nutrition where a specific supplement formulation has genuine Level I evidence for a specific genetic risk profile.

ADD1 Gly460Trp — heterozygous  genotype: TG
rs4961  ·  Chromosome 4:2906707  ·  Gene: ADD1  ·  Variant: c.1378G>T (p.Gly460Trp)  ·  CADD score: 31  ·  Freq: 20.8%  ·  OMIM: 102680.0001
Watch
ClinVar: Drug response / pathogenic (insufficient evidence) Expert panel reviewed · 2 submitters 45 PubMed publications
What this gene does: ADD1 (alpha-adducin) encodes a cytoskeletal protein expressed in kidney tubular cells. Alpha-adducin plays a regulatory role in the activity of the Na+/K+-ATPase pump — the primary transport protein responsible for sodium reabsorption from urine back into the bloodstream in the renal tubules. More sodium reabsorption means less sodium is excreted in urine, and higher sodium levels remain in the blood, driving fluid retention and elevated blood pressure.

What this variant does: The Gly460Trp substitution (glycine replaced by tryptophan at position 460) increases the activity of the Na+/K+-ATPase pump in the kidney. This results in enhanced sodium reabsorption — meaning Cathy's kidneys retain more sodium per unit of dietary intake than an average person would. The consequence is salt-sensitive hypertension: her blood pressure responds more dramatically to sodium intake than most people's does. The same meal that causes a mild transient BP rise in someone without this variant may cause a larger and longer-lasting elevation in Cathy.

Clinical context: This variant does not cause hypertension automatically — it is a susceptibility gene, not a disease gene. Many people with ADD1 Gly460Trp have normal blood pressure if they maintain a low-sodium diet. However, it means that dietary sodium management is a higher-priority cardiovascular tool for Cathy than it would be for the general population. The American Heart Association recommends under 2,300mg sodium/day for the general population; for someone with ADD1 sensitivity, a stricter target closer to 1,500mg/day may be more appropriate and should be discussed with her provider.

Pharmacogenomic relevance: ClinVar documents this variant as specifically affecting response to furosemide (a loop diuretic) and spironolactone. Carriers of the Trp460 allele demonstrate enhanced blood pressure response to these diuretics compared to Gly460 individuals — they work better. If Cathy ever requires antihypertensive medication, this genotype suggests thiazide diuretics and loop diuretics may be particularly effective choices.

Dietary counters: Potassium directly antagonizes sodium's effect at the kidney level through a separate transport mechanism. A potassium-rich diet (sweet potatoes, avocado, bananas, leafy greens, legumes, salmon) functionally blunts the ADD1 variant's sodium-retention effect. Magnesium also supports healthy vascular tone and is a natural vasodilator, complementing the blood pressure management strategy.

AMPD1 — heterozygous carrier  genotype: AG
rs17602729  ·  Chromosome 1:115236057  ·  Gene: AMPD1  ·  CADD score: 36 (uncommon)  ·  Freq: 3.8%  ·  OMIM: 102770.0001
Low-Moderate
ClinVar: Conflicting interpretations / other Criteria provided · 6 submitters 17 PubMed publications Uncommon variant (3.8% frequency) Autosomal recessive — likely carrier only
What this gene does: AMPD1 (adenosine monophosphate deaminase 1) encodes the enzyme myoadenylate deaminase, which is expressed specifically in skeletal muscle. This enzyme plays a central role in the purine nucleotide cycle — the energy management system within muscle cells. During intense exercise, when ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is rapidly consumed and AMP (adenosine monophosphate) accumulates, myoadenylate deaminase converts AMP to IMP (inosine monophosphate) and ammonia. This reaction helps regenerate ATP more efficiently and maintains the energy charge within muscle cells during high-demand activity.

What this variant does: This variant (rs17602729) causes a loss-of-function change in AMPD1. In the homozygous state (two copies), it produces myoadenylate deaminase deficiency (MMDD) — the most prevalent genetic enzyme deficiency in humans, affecting approximately 1–2% of the population. Full MMDD can manifest as exercise-induced muscle pain, weakness, premature fatigue, and occasionally rhabdomyolysis or elevated creatine kinase after exertion.

Carrier status (heterozygous — Cathy's situation): As a heterozygous carrier (one copy), Cathy has one functional and one non-functional AMPD1 gene. Because MMDD is autosomal recessive, carriers typically do not manifest the full condition. However, research suggests that heterozygous carriers may experience milder versions of exercise-related symptoms — particularly faster-than-expected muscle fatigue onset and slower recovery between exercise sessions. This is consistent with a partial reduction in muscle energy recycling efficiency.

Practical exercise implications: This is particularly relevant given the osteoporosis diagnosis — resistance training is the most evidence-backed lifestyle intervention for building and preserving bone density. The AMPD1 carrier status doesn't mean Cathy should avoid exercise, but it does mean she should expect and plan for:
  • Earlier fatigue onset than peers of similar fitness level
  • More pronounced post-exercise soreness (DOMS) and longer recovery windows
  • Potentially elevated creatine kinase after heavy sessions
  • Better results from lower-intensity, higher-frequency training than from high-intensity infrequent sessions

Cardiovascular implications: The report notes that a small percentage of homozygous-deficient individuals display increased predisposition to stress-related cardiovascular ailments including heart disease and stroke. As a carrier, this risk is attenuated, but it underscores the importance of not overexerting without adequate recovery — cardiac muscle also uses the purine nucleotide cycle under stress. CoQ10 directly supports mitochondrial ATP production efficiency and is the most targeted supplement intervention for this variant.

Note on CADD score 36: A CADD score of 36 places this variant in the top ~0.04% of potentially deleterious variants in the genome — indicating this is a structurally meaningful change even though it's recessive. The 3.8% population frequency makes it "uncommon" but not rare.

rs7412  ·  Chromosome 19:45412079  ·  Gene: APOE  ·  Variant: c.526C>T (p.Arg176Cys)  ·  CADD score: 25.1  ·  Freq: 7.5%  ·  OMIM: 107741.0001

ClinVar: Drug response (generally protective) Expert panel reviewed · 4 submitters 304 PubMed publications Homozygous — two copies of protective variant
What this gene does: APOE (apolipoprotein E) encodes a protein that plays a central role in lipid metabolism — specifically in the transport and clearance of cholesterol, triglycerides, and other lipids through the bloodstream and lymphatic system. ApoE binds to lipid particles (VLDL, chylomicron remnants) and acts as a ligand for hepatic LDL receptors, facilitating their clearance from circulation. ApoE is also expressed in the brain, where it plays a critical role in neuronal repair, synaptic plasticity, and clearing amyloid-beta protein — the primary constituent of Alzheimer's disease plaques.

The three APOE variants — context for ApoE2: The APOE gene has three major variants (alleles): ApoE2 (rs7412 T allele), ApoE3 (the most common), and ApoE4 (the high-risk allele). ApoE4 is the most studied genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's disease — one copy doubles risk; two copies increases risk 8–12x. ApoE2 is at the opposite end: it is associated with significantly reduced Alzheimer's risk compared to ApoE3, and dramatically lower risk than ApoE4.

Cathy's ApoE2/E2 genotype (homozygous TT): Carrying two copies of the ApoE2 allele is rare — only about 0.5–1% of the population is ApoE2/E2. This is Cathy's most genetically favorable finding. ApoE2/E2 individuals have meaningfully reduced amyloid-beta accumulation in the brain compared to ApoE3/E3 or any ApoE4 combination. The mechanism is that ApoE2 binds amyloid-beta more effectively than ApoE4, facilitating its clearance before it can form the plaques associated with Alzheimer's pathology. While this does not make someone immune to Alzheimer's (which has many genetic and environmental contributors), it represents a significant biological advantage.

The lipid monitoring caveat: ApoE2 has lower affinity for LDL receptors compared to ApoE3 or ApoE4. In the general population this means ApoE2 individuals tend to have lower LDL cholesterol — another favorable finding. However, homozygous ApoE2/E2 can in some individuals be associated with familial type 3 hyperlipoproteinemia (also called familial dysbetalipoproteinemia or remnant particle disease). This condition occurs when ApoE2/E2 combines with other genetic or environmental factors (obesity, hypothyroidism, diabetes, certain medications) to cause marked elevation of both cholesterol and triglycerides from accumulation of VLDL remnants. It does not occur in all ApoE2/E2 individuals — only those with additional metabolic stressors — but it must be monitored with a fasting lipid panel since it is not visible on the current CBC panel.

Statin pharmacogenomics: ClinVar documents atorvastatin response (efficacy) as a drug response variant for APOE. ApoE2 individuals tend to have a better LDL-lowering response to statins than ApoE4 individuals. If a statin is ever prescribed for lipid management, this is favorable context — she is likely to respond well.

What this means practically: This is a genuinely good finding and should be shared with Cathy clearly. The cognitive lifestyle habits that support brain health — quality sleep, aerobic exercise, social connection, stress management, intellectual engagement — remain important regardless of genotype, but she has a favorable genetic backdrop for neurological aging. The priority this finding creates is simply a fasting lipid panel to confirm the lipid picture is normal, and then annual monitoring thereafter.
